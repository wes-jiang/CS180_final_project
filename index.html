Final Project 1: Augmented Reality

Setup

I found a flat surface to place, in this case, a cereal box, and drew a a regular pattern across the box. Then I proceeded to label roughly 60 points across the box using ‘ginput’ : each points corresponds to a (u,v) in image coordinates and an (x,y,z) in world frame coordinates. For each point that was manually label, correspond them to it’s respective world frame coordinate.

Propagating Keypoints to other Images in the Video: Off the Shelf Trackers

I used some ‘off the shelf trackers’ to track each point on the box. To do this, I initialized a tracker for each point. For each frame, I updated the trackers, kept track of their positions, and found the 3d to 2d transformation matrix T, using least squares on the 3d points shown and their corresponding 2d points. Then for each frame I multiplied the 3d corners points of the cube with the transformation to get image coordinates (u,v). With these image coordinates, I plotted a cube for each frame using OpenCV libraries.

Performing least squares on this system should produce the 3x4 matrix T!

Different trackers produce different results! For median tracker, some of the trackers seem to ‘lose track’ of their dots, which is probably why the cube looks a bit wonky. So I tried a few more based on the trackers listed on this website. The second tracker I used is called ‘boosting’ - and it does quite a good job tracking dots, except it mis-tracks dots as the camera moves. Nevertheless the cube is a little more stable. CSRT tracker by far produces the best results.

Summary: What did I learn?

This project was a great way to learn how 3d to 2d transformations work. Using only least squares I was able to understand ‘a change of frames’ where I can firstly: estimate a transformation matrix, and secondly transform 3d coordinates into image coordinates to draw virtual 3d objects. This idea can be taken to the next step perhaps with non-geometric objects like a light bulb or a simple bowl. It was a nice introduction to how basic augmented reality works.

Final Project 2: Lightfield Camera

Depth Refocusing:

Objects far away from the camera don’t vary in position as much when the camera moves around where as the objects much closer to the camera will move much more when the camera moves. By shifting the images based on the center in which we will call (c1, c2) and the shifts given by the image file (u, v), I am able to align the images via averaging and applying different alpha values to change the depth of focus simply by scaling the difference between the center (c1, c2) and the shifts (u,v) by alpha: alpha*(center - shifts). Aperture Adjustment:

The previous section’s blurring mechanism came from averaging the non-aligned areas of images post shift. In this section, we will be simulating toggling aperture size to vary the focus of the image. Implementing this is very similar to the the previous section, except, instead of taking the mean of the entire set of images, you take the mean of fewer images that meet a new condition: that the distance between the camera coordinates from the central image is within a set radius. Filter the image set under this new condition, and taking the mean of the images that meet the condition. The resulting image will simulate a camera lens w/ varying aperture.

What went wrong?

Both depth refocusing and aperture readjustment didn’t seem to work no matter how hard I tried to reimplement the code. This is probably due to my setup - I simply didn’t have the right tools to take the correct photos in the right positions. Ideally, I should have a gantry, or a tripod where I could carefully shift my images to photos like how the archives did.

Unfortunately, I only could shift my camera with my hands, which produced unideal uncalibrated images, which explains all the blurriness. Perhaps a way I could have improved this is by taking a stream of photos.

Summary: What did I learn?

The light field camera — is able to focus on what I want to focus on given the right shifts in images. This is because the light field camera is able to capture lot of information about light rays coming into the lens. This behavior can be simulated by taking the aligning carefully shifted images as with this project. I believe this was a good way to learn about databases of images can be post-processed to replicate camera functionality.
